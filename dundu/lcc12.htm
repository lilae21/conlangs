Conlanging to reflect on society: feelings, worldview and kindness through Dundulanyä vocabulary and idioms

Transcription of my presentation for the 12th Language Creation Conference (LCC12) in Copenhagen, Denmark, July 10-12, 2026.
Dundulanyä main page Bibliography Transcript

This is a sort of addendum to the Author's notes and thoughts page, as most of these concepts are already covered there. Some back story to this: in early 2026 I saw the call for proposals for the twelfth edition of the Language Creation Conference, to be held in Copenhagen, Denmark, from July 10 to July 12, 2026. It was around March and I was a bit skeptical, as I hadn't interacted much with the conlanging community in years, but especially during 2025 I had developed some pretty intense ideas about the development of Dundulanyä – the ones detailed in the aforementioned page – which I wanted to share.

At first, however, I wasn't very sure as it was going to be a pretty personal presentation and didn't think it could be very interesting. Until I happened to talk about it to a very close friend of mine, an artist although not a conlanger, and after chatting about the topic for a bit I decided, why not, I could try to propose this presentation of mine.

Eventually it got accepted into the LCC12 program and I pre-recorded a ten minute video, aired during the morning session of Saturday, July 11, 2026. Here in this page is the transcription of the presentation (some small details might differ, but this was the script I read), with an additional paragraph (the one about the root √homf-) which I cut from the video in order to fit into the ten minute slot I had (and I just fit at 9 minutes and 56 seconds, by the way). I also added some links to things I reference, and the bibliography which was one of the slides.

Transcript

Hello everyone! First of all, I'd like to thank the organizers of this twelfth edition of the Language Creation Conference for putting up such a program and, personally, for having accepted my proposal for a presentation.

The topic I decided to focus on is Conlanging to reflect on society: feelings, worldview and kindness through Dundulanyä vocabulary and idioms, and I'm mostly going to talk about the conlanging process, about conlangs as works of art and how they reflect the inner world of their creators. I'm going to focus more about the artistic side of conlanging, as that means applying to this form of art, and my own creative experience, that kind of perspective I'm used to apply to other forms of art due to my background.

I often cite non-conlanging and non-linguistic inspirations when I talk about my conlangs, and Dundulanyä most prominently: for example, the works of art by Niki de Saint Phalle; the photographs of Lisetta Carmi, lyrics and music by Joni Mitchell. Artists who have a peculiar gaze on the world which particularly resonates with me and, well, inspire me to aim to convey that feeling through conlanging. In fact I think I started doing that even before I explicitely wrote that this was my goal. I eventually realized that the line between the two common labels artlangs and heartlangs, in my work, was a lot more blurred than I thought — I prefer to use artlang in a wider way, art as the expression of something very deep within its author. That, as well as a means to reflect on our own society, to uncover and challenge bias.

So, that said, how does Dundulanyä, and the act of presenting it, reflect all this? Etymology, meanings of words and idioms are those areas where the feelings and the worldview of its speakers emerge the most — and here it's less important the fact that it's also mine. Last year I was eventually stunned by a realization I had. I have a personal website where I put my conlanging work on and last year I was using AI to troubleshoot technical issues and help me in things like scripting. One day, I was brainstorming about how to create a searchable dictionary and asked an AI chatbot to help me with JavaScript. I didn't input any actual word, so in creating the technical solution it made up two example words with the following meanings: "formal greeting" and "honored warrior". These were the first things an AI chatbot produced as outputs — not "tree", nor "hand", nor "water", but "honored warrior". I was puzzled as I already had in mind everything I've said before about kindness and the worldview, and to see "honored warrior" as one of the first examples shook me: this tells something about the conlangs it used as training material, and yet another proof, this time conlanging-related, of how easy it is for AI to reflect our own society's bias, seeing war – the most extreme example of violence – as a source of honor.

This shocked me as the very first time I started actively revising my conlang to incorporate more of the concepts I mentioned before was after reading an extremely interesting book by German historian, anthropologist and feminist Heide Goettner-Abendroth, on matriarchal societies of the European Paleolithic. Among a plethora of things I learned from that book, there was a reflection on taboos, and I almost immediately applied it to my conlanging work, to Dundulanyä, in a particular way: the main taboo was to be violence, and the field of vocabulary where this is clear is insults: our own society has most of the worst slurs being related to female sexual promiscuity, even when insulting men, and deviation from male-centric standards, I wanted the Dundulanyä to have violence and lack of empathy as taboo instead. There are words such as ṣogērṣe, which means roughly "one who punches", or skartibe, literally "brawl", which are slurs. Even worse than violence is clear lack of empathy. A word which is a very strong insult is noṇḍūmeśe, a univerbation of the phrase noṇḍū meśah, meaning "I see their shoulders", implying somebody who turns away in a moment of need. Then, perhaps the worst slur is viṣoṣū, which, although with a different etymology, means "cold-hearted". In English it is not positive but certainly not an insult. In Dundulanyä, it is the very negation of someone's human nature — the lack of empathy for another person, an inability to feel.

Another particular field where the worldview is particularly represented is etymologies related to the extension to the human sphere of concepts that are more concretely related to nature. The co-dependence between humans and nature – time is limited so I'll only imply the ramifications of such an analysis to our Earth, today – is particularly meaningful in Dundulanyä society and their view often shows, if not zero, few signs of anthropocentrism. There's a whole bunch of words for body parts that makes parallels between humans and plants: ṇīṭah is both the skin, of a human and of any animal, and a tree's bark. A "hearing leaf", hindnūlya, is an ear; a b̃oṭa is a finger and a petal. A word I particularly like could be translated as "hero" or "heroine": saṃlulya, which however comes from the same root as lulya "flower" - it means "that who blooms forth". Heroines and heroes in Dundulanyä are not that particular kind of hero we'd think of - they're a more Le Guin-esque kind of hero, and that word also is often used as "wunderkind" or something like that. Speaking about courage, and especially strength, heroic traits which Western languages inevitably link to masculinity... well, in Dundulanyä there's a word for someone of exceptional strength and capabilities... and it's kiṅkṣelīsamamū, literally "mother of three twins".

Vocabulary for emotions, and in particular the act of sharing feelings is quite developed, often tied to just a few roots. The root √homf- means "to blush" and is linked to closeness and friendship, such as udhihumfa a cozy, heart-warming feeling in a moment that makes someone happy and at peace — udhi- is a prefix roughly meaning "within"; sehumfa – with the prefix se-, meaning "with" – is a word for "friendship"; homfiḫe is a compliment, but mostly an endearing one, as it is closeness and mutual help what the Dundulanyä value the most.

As a conclusion to this presentation, I'd like to point out something which is not about the conlang itself, but rather on the way I share it. Example texts and sentences are very important to me not just to show off the linguistic features of a conlang but also to set the tones, to share meanings — in some ways, I'd like to think of my conlang's grammar as a part of the work of art itself. Given that, to me, my conlangs are tightly bound to the conworld they're set in, first of all I try to make examples as environmentally and culturally coherent as possible: it's a different planet, sure, but by trying to use the closest Earth analogs there are lots of papayas, guavas, and monsonic rains in my examples rather than apples or heavy snowfall. But that applies to the cultural side too. Dundulanyä started out as a major revision of my previous main conlang, which was called Chlouvānem – if some of you happened to be in Facebook groups around 2018-2019 you might have heard of it – so many example sentences were meant to be the same. Except that I decided to adapt them more, as I wanted examples that showed kindness, caring, more positive feelings without ending up doing something too cheesy or naïfve. To show off an absolute construction, for Chlouvānem in 2018 I came up with this example sentence: "[Seeing his/her] driver in the gravel, the team principal got angry", clearly influenced by the fact I was following motorsports a lot back then, but still anger was the feeling that was most prominent in this example; when writing this section of the Dundulanyä grammar in 2025 such a sentence was unacceptable for me, I went in the complete opposite direction and wrote: "[Reading] the heart-warming message, the boy suddenly smiled." So there are heart-warming messages, paintings that move someone to tears, small things done to help. More caring teachers, instead of honored warriors. There's that short essay by Ursula K. Le Guin, The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction, which goes a lot deeper than this, but when I first read it I realized it was in some way what I had already in mind when I was documenting Dundulanyä. And while to this day I haven't written any culturally specific text about Dundulanyä, I didn't want my example texts to be the usual translations of the Babel story and the such — I chose a short children's tale by Italian author Gianni Rodari called "Brif, bruf, braf", so inherently happy and cheerful and about two children creating their own secret language, so that it felt like the perfect text to showcase a conlang in, and especially Dundulanyä.

So this is it — I hope you enjoyed this brief presentation of my approach to conlanging and these few features of Dundulanyä. Thanks again to the organizers and thank you all for following!
