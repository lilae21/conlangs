Caveat lector

Elodian is a conlang that I have been doubting whether to put into this website. It is an a posteriori language that, arguably, has become the most developed a posteriori language I've ever made, even more than Atlantic, my 2019-2020 project that you can find as a divertissement like this one. Yet, not only I've never felt Elodian to be that complete, but for more than two and a half years – this version of Elodian was started in July 2022, as shown by my sketches and sound changes file on my computer that is still called lif722.odt, "lif" after Lifashian, the previous version's name, and "722" is the month and year – I have felt the need to flesh it out more.

In some ways, in the moment I'm writing this preface, I still think so. Unlike Lifashian, its predecessor, for two and a half years I've been procrastinating fleshing out Elodian grammar. Granted, soon after starting it I got deep into real life events and when I found myself seriously conlanging again it was time for Dundulanyä to take the spotlight. Elodian is a really large project, but with tens of pages worth of conworlding (alt-history) material, and barely three pages of grammar, before detailing it for this page.

Because of that and because of my ever dwindling inclination of making conlangs set in the very same world of ours, something I mentioned in the Author's notes about Dundulanyä, I was skeptical about making a page for Elodian.

Yet, in early March 2025 I opened the spreadsheet with the Elodian dictionary – from time to time I still add some words, more as an exercise in creative etymology – and I saw that there are 3028 entries. Somehow I thought they're too many to let this project, which at the time was the third-longest conlang project of mine – and now exactly second-longest after Dundulanyä, having overtaken even the latter's previous version, Chlouvānem – just rot on my hard disk. Still it took me another year to actually feel comfortable sharing it, mostly due to the ever-present skepticism about the ethics of an alternate-Earth conlangs.

Oh, and a bonus, if you're into condialects: the Elodian project actually includes a condialect of Ligurian, Pontic Ligurian, which Elodian takes words from!

Unlike Atlantic, the other a posteriori divertissement, whose grammar was entirely written when it was a current project, this time I actually wrote most of the grammar and examples specifically for this site. Just to let Elodian be something more than just a 3000+ word naming language. I don't really plan to continue it the way it is, still, enjoy this trip!

— Lilaë, July 2026

Introduction

Elodian, natively referred to as ɂelodīru or (la) gulkā ɂelodīrā, is an Indo-European language, an isolate inside the family, spoken in an alternate timeline of Earth in the northeastern corner of Asia Minor, i.e. the historical region of Pontus and neighboring areas across the Pontic Alps into the Armenian highlands. It is the official language of the republic of ʔelodīhūto, spoken by the majority of its population. Elodian is the native language of about fifteen million people in the world, the majority of which in ʔelodīhūto, with smaller communities in Eastern Turkey, Syria, Iran and Iraq.

Elodian developed on its own, distinctly from other Indo-European languages, although it is definitely closer to the Anatolian languages, particularly the Luwian subgroup, than to other languages in the family, despite sharing some traits with Armenian and Greek; some linguists, especially Elodian ones, refer to it as a member of an areal grouping, the Elodian-Anatolian languages.

It is particularly noteworthy due to its system of split ergativity, which makes it virtually an ergative-absolutive language (although not syntactically ergative) except with first- and second-person referents, which require a nominative-accusative alignment.

Its vocabulary has a substantial number of inherited roots, but through millennia the language absorbed many loanwords, especially from Persian and Arabic (through the former), and to smaller extents from its neighbours Armenian, the Kartvelian languages and Turkish, as well as from Greek and Russian. Long-term Genoese supremacy and settlement of the area, and reciprocal contacts also introduced many Ligurian loans, as well as forming one of the main ethnic minorities in the country, Pontic Ligurians, which had a marked influence on the culture of coastal urban areas and held virtually all political power in the country until 1971.

It is written in the Elodian alphabet, a bicameral script ultimately related to other ancient scripts of Asia Minor like the Lydian alphabet.

History

Elodian is an Indo-European languages which is an isolate within the larger family, although it shares some features with Indo-Iranian, Armenian and Greek and many others, including some lexical similarities, with Anatolian. Elodian linguists tend to consider it an early split from post-Anatolian Common Indo-European.

Elodian is unique among Indo-European languages as it has three distinct consonantal reflexes for the palatovelars, velars, and labiovelars (hence it is both or neither centum nor satem) and, even more notably, three distinct consonantal reflexes for all three laryngeals. It also exhibit a consonant shift analogue to Grimm's law or to Proto-Armenian, with a few complexities.

Language contact

One of the most striking features of Elodian is its extensive history of language contact, which is a direct consequence of the area's history. Through its history at the crossroads of Europe and Asia and the long contact between languages resulting from the millennia through which Elodians were always subjects of foreign powers, the vocabulary of Elodian has been enriched and shaped by the absorption of plenty of loanwords, which certainly dates back to Elodian prehistory. The vast majority of loanwords, also, are considered to be fully integrated in the language, helped by the fact that all of them (except some proper nouns) are transcribed into the Elodian script and are adapted to fit native morphology. It is also likely that some words in current Elodian were borrowed from ancient dialects or closely related languages that have not survived, as shown by a few examples of PIE *p in Elodian.

The share of loanwords is not uniform in the Elodian vocabulary. First of all, the vast majority of borrowed words in any stage of the language are nouns, and the number of borrowed adjectives with no corresponding noun, while not small on its own, is far smaller. Borrowed verbs are few and rare, as Elodian has only two suffixes that form verbs from other parts of speech – -ez(ām) and -haz(ēm), originally two contextual variants of the same PIE root – already attested in the very first Elodian attestations in the 6th century CE; even those suffixes generally stopped being productive by the 15th century, replaced in productivity by compound verbs, with new compound verbs sometimes even replacing full verbs; the verbal part of compound verbs, which contributes little meaning of its own, is nearly always a native root. Verbal roots loaned from other languages are even rarer and all of them date to the prehistory of the Elodian language, being generally of Hurro-Urartian, Akkadian or Kartvelian origin.

Moreover, loanwords are not evenly distributed in terms of frequency; all function words are native, inherited from Proto-Indo-European, as are many of the most commonly used words, so that the most basic forms of the language contain mostly native roots. However, very basic words are not always native, sometimes due to semantic drift that has caused loanwords to fit into the other meaning. Almost as a counterpoint to basic words being mostly inherited roots, words such as those for "man" and "woman" are borrowed, as are nearly all Elodian given names: most of them from Middle Persian, but also from Kartvelian and Northwest Caucasian languages, while Western names are typically borrowed through Medieval Greek or through Ligurian. Islamic theophoric names are borrowed from Arabic.

AThe most ancient layer of loanwords dates back to Elodian prehistory, and it is probably the most represented layer of loanwords in non-technical speech. Such words date back from the arrival of Elodians in eastern Anatolia up to the first few centuries AD (roughly up until the influx of Christianity shown by Aramaic loanwords and the Parthian period); prehistoric loanwords generally include words of multiple origin: Akkadian, Hurro-Urartian, Anatolian, Proto-Iranian, Hattic, Kartvelian or from other Caucasian languages. Nearly all of proposed etymologies attributed to these languages belong to (or originally entered the language in) the semantic fields of animals, plants, agriculture, and early technology; some examples are šegar "beer", imēš "amber", tǝkǝ̄t "soap" (from Akkadian), eyn "God, Goddess, divinity", šol "group", norāt "pomegranate", waṛā "countryside", probably neht "bed" (from Hurrian), howā "valley", šǝlūr "plum", hēr "road" (attributed to Urartian). Quite distinct are however some very basic nouns which have been borrowed, such as turǝniš "man" from Hurrian, and Akkadian borrowings particularly include concepts such as somor "music", nesfǝr "message", tuwfā "book", or gexšorā "plan, scheme". There are furthermore words such as zižǝn "smile", ğuygā "juniper", šewnī "beautiful", somlart "medlar" or toğoy "mouse", all with proposed Kartvelian cognates; žūto "owl", božāmā "wasp", xafrōšā "butterfly", loncā "maple" or yǝyjā "ash", all of likely Northwest Caucasian origin, especially with cognates in Ubykh and Abkhaz; as well as toponyms related to these and other languages: for example the name Fulahmīwā of the capital city of ʔelodīhūto has been traced back to Hattic goddess Furušemu. The names of the first six planets, excluding Earth – Nōw, Istērā, Nilkēri, Morlōk, Nenōrt – are also likely Akkadian-mediated loans of ancient Mesopotamian words, which, through interpretatio Elodica, form the basis of the names for five out of seven days of the week. Words generally attributed to Indo-European Anatolian languages are most likely to be influenced, areal, or calques, as the phonological shape does not always support the hypothesis of direct borrowing. A few dozen words are generally categorized as Iranian loans, being either early loans from Proto-Iranian or mediated by other unspecified languages: such words include gōb(ām) "to read" – one of a few borrowed verbs – calīr "splendour" (likely from *ćriHrás and therefore cognate with Sanskrit śrī), xašrā "world", mirǝ̄jē "rice" (possibly from Elamite through an Iranian language) or howftor "better".

The largest share of loanwords into Elodian comes from Persian, including words borrowed from Parthian and Middle (Sasanian) Persian, Classical Persian, and a very small number of words from contemporary Iranian Persian. Persian loanwords are found in every semantic field, from many everyday words (sālong "easy", gēsi "hair", mōm "candle", ronggi "color"); words related to general urban and political life (šahrestān "province", moydān "square", marzā "border, limit"); knowledge (lars "lesson"; lānešn "knowledge"); agriculture (lōxt "field", gozor "parsnip", konjǝl "sesame"), to more abstract concepts (lēnā "religion", sorūl "anthem"), and even some derivational affixes (-īft, -ēšn) and prepositions (bo "towards"). More abstract concepts, ethnonyms, and Islam-related words come from Arabic but in the vast majority of cases they entered Elodian through Persian, so they are usually considered of Persian origin too; such words include e.g. dakīkā "minute", haylā "family", mǝlhabi "stadium", harzi "latitude, parallel" and tūl "longitude, meridian", nesf "hemisphere", ɂentefāzā "rebellion", sāhebi "companion, colleague", seyǝ̄sā "politics".

Dating back to the first millennium CE are also likely most Armenian loans, which also cover many semantic fields, but more everyday words than Persian loans; they include a few common verbs such as gurē(m) "to write" and sirē(m) "to like", but also e.g. ōrēn "rule; law", zōr "power", tolay "boy", nong or yong "yes", ōdang "gas", and even more derivational suffixes such as -ǝyā, -c and possibly -ngong. Also from the early first millennium (around the time of the earliest attestations of Elodian) are the Aramaic loans, introduced alongside Syriac Christianity and generally limited to that semantic field, such as kodīš "(Christian) saint", ināš "human", pošītā "Peshitta — more generally the Bible for any Christian denomination", sǝlōtā "prayer", Išoh Mošiho "Jesus Christ"; there are also a few Aramaic loans not strictly related to Christianity, such as tewfā "box", potihā "watermelon" or lap "paper".

Greek loanwords belong to two layers: an earlier one with more varied semantic fields (zof "darkness", ninfā "woman", kal "jar", līlā "afternoon", kurfā "helmet", sotālo "stadion; (colloquially) two hundred meters") and a later one, generally used in scientific terms, but using a sound correspondence that hints to the original (ancient) loanwords (epatā "hallucination", linosawr "dinosaur", or German-mediated owtomot "ATM").

During the Middle Ages and the Early Modern Era, and – with few sporadic exceptions – up to 1971 the language of the ruling economical, and most recently political, élite was Ligurian, and many words entered Elodian from it, either from the more "rural" variety closer to Intemelio of most settlers, or from the more "refined" speech closer to Genoese of the élite. Many of these relate to administration or commerce (palāng "money", dyugangā "customs", awǝntaji "profit, gain", bitēgā "shop", mazanggīng "warehouse", purpuzisyung "bill (legislation proposal)"), nautical terms (bekezi "pitching"), but also quite a few general words (bunamang "tip (monetary)", cǝ̄w "OK", turna "again") and some foodstuffs (galetā "(salted) biscuit", pǝrbujūng "pasta- or dough filling from mostly spontaneous herbs, or a vegetable soup"). Some post-1500 loanwords from Romance languages and English also show the intermediate stage of Pontic Ligurian, such as retiyātā "retirement", built in Pontic Ligurian as a cognate to French retraite. In turn, Elodian has loaned plenty of contemporary words to Pontic Ligurian, resulting in the latter diverging in vocabulary from European Ligurian.

Words from other languages – mainly Turkish, Russian, French, more recently English – are rarer. In fact, throughout the 20th century and particularly since the Emancipation – the ethnic riots of 1969-1971 which terminated the de facto hegemony of Pontic Ligurians over the country and led to the appointment of the first ethnically Elodian head of state in recorded history – newly coined Elodian words and particularly calques have been the predominant form of enriching the language's lexicon: over 90% of new entries in Elodian dictionaries since 1980 have been calques, either partial or complete. Some calques are new coinings (cf. xazi rasmok "comic", calque of Fr. bande dessinée, or suhitmā "committee, council", a calque of Greek συνέδριον), while some are meaning extensions of preexisting words, sometimes loans, e.g. moydān "square" extended to "forum" or robbān "(ship) captain" extended to "Internet browser". There are also a few phonosemantic matches such as bǝndǝ̄dā "band-aid", the first part of which is from English band- while the matching part corresponds to the ending of zemǝ̄dā "bandage".

The sound [ɲ] exists in all Elodian dialects, but can always be analyzed as the sequence /nj/, as shown by the orthographical representation ny.

The sequence /ll/ is realized as a voiceless lateral fricative [ɬː] in the majority of dialects, and as a lateral affricate [t͡ɬ] in most Eastern and Highland dialects. Non-geminate /l/ itself is fricativized to [ɬ] before voiceless stops and as its voiced equivalent [ɮ] before voiced stops; this change is prominent in most Elodian dialects with the exception of some Southwestern ones (Sebastean and Eudoxiopolitan), the mostly second-language dialect spoken in Pontic Liguria and particularly (even among natives) in inland areas such as around Äto Zenêize in the West and around Toàgna and Villa Souli in the East, and in the contemporary sociolect of Multicultural Helenopolitan Elodian.

Coda /h/ is generally silent in most dialects – leading to a number of homographs such as šar "turn, round, manche" and šahr "city (mostly in borrowings and toponyms)" – with the exception of particularly conservative ones such as in mountain areas of Hūtihaštā, Sohrābešahr, Usaraji and Māmekābādi provinces.

The sequences /st/ and /ʃt/ merge into [ʃt] virtually everywhere except for the Southeast. Orthography maintains the distinction.

Standard Elodian has a simple twelve vowel system, distinguishing short and long versions of six different vowels.

Syntax

Word order

Elodian is moderately head-initial, with adjectives, including numerals, following nouns, prepositions, relative clauses following their head, although with a SOV word order and mostly suffixing morphology.

Split ergativity

Elodian has a peculiar system of split ergativity, possibly directly influenced by contact between the Proto-Elodians and speakers of ergative Hurro-Urartian languages, as the ergative noun marker has been proposed as being borrowed from Hurrian. The nominative case is actually a nominative-absolutive case.

The split occurs based on person: sentences with a first- or second person argument are nominative-accusative, while those with a third person only are ergative-absolutive:

lu tolay lāš xwelōniheš sūš ɂenjehorzo.
lu tolay lāš xwelōn-ih-eš sūš ɂenjehorz-o
DEF.M.SG.ABS. boy.ABS.SG. DEF.F.SG.ERG. female_friend-F-ERG.SG. REFL.POSS.F.SG.ERG. hug-PAST.3SG.IND.
/lu tɔˈlaj la:ʃ ˈxwelo:ni:ʃ ˌsu:ʃ ʔenˈdʒehɔrzɔ/
The boy was hugged by his friend.
